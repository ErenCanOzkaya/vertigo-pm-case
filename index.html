Case Study — Question One

Q1 — First-Time User Experience

Game Strongest Point Weakest Point
Critical Strike Gameplay quality, seamless core loop (unconsciously starts next match) IAP popup overload at session start
Modern Ops No IAP pressure, clean session start Long match duration, low visual polish
Bullet Echo Unique top-down mechanic, smooth onboarding Feels like a puzzle game rather than an FPS, limited mobility vs competitors
Tacticool Detailed progression teaching App Store visuals vs actual gameplay mismatch, tutorial locks player for 10–15 min
Modern Strike Best visual quality and weapon feel Extra download on first launch, excessive loading screens, trivially easy first matches

Critical Strike

Critical Strike's strongest asset is its familiarity. For players who grew up with Counter-Strike, the game delivers the feeling of playing a beloved title in a remastered mobile form rather than starting something new. The core loop is exceptionally well-executed: the match pacing and session flow are engaging enough that players frequently start a new match without consciously deciding to, which is one of the strongest retention signals a mobile game can produce. However, the first-time experience is significantly undermined by IAP popup overload. Being confronted with 5 to 10 purchase prompts before playing a single match signals that the game is built to extract money rather than deliver entertainment. An additional missed opportunity is the loading screen shown to first-time players: rather than an active season video that means nothing to someone who has never played, a first-time player would benefit far more from gameplay tips, mechanics explanations, and a sense of what the game has to offer.

Improvement

The current IAP strategy is counterproductive. Flooding players with purchase prompts at session start trains them to dismiss offers without reading, making each offer less visible and less effective over time. A more effective approach would introduce IAP in three stages: no offers at all in the first few sessions, letting the player build genuine attachment to the game; a single well-timed offer after a few matches, framed as the game being on the player's side; and from that point forward, a low-frequency, behavior-triggered offer system rather than session-start bombardment.

Modern Ops

Modern Ops gets one thing right that Critical Strike does not: there is no IAP pressure at session start, and the transition from tutorial to first match is fast and frictionless. However, the first match runs significantly longer than competing titles, and since early matches are intentionally easy, the result is a low-stakes, drawn-out experience that exhausts patience before any real attachment can form. The deeper problem is identity: every visual element feels like a first draft of a generic FPS rather than a deliberate design decision. The game does not feel like Modern Ops. It feels like a game called FPS Game, which makes it very difficult for players to feel any specific attachment to it.

Improvement

Introduce shorter match formats for early sessions to create a faster play-win loop suited to mobile session lengths. More importantly, invest in a stronger visual identity and a more distinctive art direction to give players a reason to feel attached to Modern Ops specifically rather than treating it as an interchangeable shooter.

Bullet Echo

Bullet Echo is a genuinely different product: a top-down stealth shooter where enemies are only visible within line of sight, played in maze-like environments. This is a real design strength, but it also creates the game's biggest first-time experience risk. Players who download expecting a traditional FPS encounter something that feels closer to a puzzle game, with limited mobility and a completely different competitive dynamic. The onboarding itself is smooth and match length is appropriate, but no tutorial can compensate for a fundamental mismatch between what the App Store implies and what the game actually delivers.

Improvement

Clarify the game's unique genre positioning both in App Store visuals and in the onboarding flow. New players should understand before their first match that they are entering a top-down stealth shooter with puzzle-like elements, not a traditional FPS. Leaning into the game's uniqueness rather than trying to appeal to the broader FPS audience would attract a more loyal and better-matched player base.

Tacticool

Tacticool's most damaging first-time experience problem begins before the game is even downloaded. The App Store screenshots create a strong FPS impression, while the actual game is a top-down third-person shooter with a slower pace and a fundamentally different mechanic. Players who arrive expecting one thing and encounter another are primed to disengage before the game has had a chance to show its strengths. The tutorial compounds this: rather than releasing the player after a single introductory match, it guides them through 4 to 5 consecutive forced missions, keeping new players locked in a structured loop for 10 to 15 minutes.

Improvement

Redesign the tutorial to release players into free play after a single introductory match, delivering upgrade and progression guidance through optional tooltips rather than forced mission sequences. More critically, update App Store visuals to accurately reflect the game's top-down third-person mechanics, as the current screenshots generate avoidable disappointment from the very first session.

Modern Strike

Modern Strike is the most visually polished game in this comparison. Weapon models, character animations, and overall production quality are noticeably superior to competing titles. However, the first-time experience is weakened by several structural issues. The game requires an additional download after the initial install. Once in the game, first matches are excessively easy: enemies die in a single hit regardless of weapon. Scoring 25 kills in a first match without dying once is not empowering, it is meaningless. Finally, when a match ends, the screen offers only an exit option rather than a prominent play-again button, which breaks the momentum at exactly the moment a player is most primed to continue.

Improvement

Calibrate first match difficulty to feel challenging but winnable. Add a prominent play-again button to the post-match screen, as the moment after a match ends is the highest-engagement point in a session. Reduce loading screen frequency between matches, as the current wait times actively interrupt the session momentum that keeps players engaged.

Part B — D1 / D7 / D30 Churn Hypotheses

Critical Strike +

D1 — Day 1 Churn

The primary D1 churn driver is IAP popup overload at session start. Upon opening the app, players are confronted with 5 to 10 purchase prompts before reaching the main menu or playing a single match. This creates two compounding problems: first, it signals that the game is built to extract money rather than deliver entertainment, eroding motivation before gameplay even begins. Second, the sheer volume of popups trains players to dismiss them without reading, making this an ineffective monetization strategy in addition to a retention risk. One US reviewer described spending five minutes closing popups before uninstalling immediately, never having played a match. A secondary D1 driver is the cluttered home screen: between the IAP popups, the volume of menu options, and the general visual noise, new players struggle to orient themselves and understand what to do next.

D7 — Day 7 Churn

By day 7, the pay-to-win structure becomes impossible to ignore. Players who do not spend money face opponents with dramatically superior weapons and health pools, a gap that becomes viscerally frustrating when a player lands multiple accurate hits, including headshots, only to be killed by a few return shots from a better-equipped opponent. This breaks the fundamental contract of a skill-based FPS: the feeling that performance reflects ability. User reviews support this strongly, with 285 combined TR and US mentions of pay-to-win and IAP-related complaints, the highest of any game in this analysis.

D30 — Day 30 Churn

Long-term churn is driven by the realization that meaningful progression without spending is structurally blocked. Reviews from players with 1 to 3 months of playtime describe hitting a hard paywall where gear gaps become insurmountable and every match outcome feels predetermined by spending level. A secondary D30 driver is content stagnation: players who have exhausted available game modes and maps without a new season or content update lose their reason to return.

Modern Ops +

D1 — Day 1 Churn

The primary D1 churn driver is match length. Unlike Critical Strike, Modern Ops matches run significantly longer from the very first session. Long matches against easy bot opponents create a low-stakes, drawn-out experience that feels more like a chore than entertainment. A secondary D1 driver is presentation quality: the interface feels underdeveloped and visually low-effort, creating a perception of lower product quality.

D7 — Day 7 Churn

By day 7, bot-heavy matchmaking becomes the dominant frustration. Players begin to realize that a significant portion of their opponents are bots, which hollows out the competitive motivation that PvP games depend on. Winning against bots stops feeling like an achievement. A secondary D7 driver is the lack of progression clarity: the game does not clearly communicate what players are working toward.

D30 — Day 30 Churn

Long-term churn is driven by ad monetization fatigue. User reviews describe ad lengths increasing over time, with one long-time player citing the move to two-minute ads as the breaking point. This is particularly damaging: the players most affected are the most engaged free-to-play users, meaning Modern Ops risks churning its most loyal non-paying segment.

Bullet Echo +

D1 — Day 1 Churn

Bullet Echo creates a significant D1 churn risk driven by audience mismatch. Players who download expecting a traditional FPS encounter a top-down stealth shooter with puzzle-like elements. No tutorial can overcome a fundamental mismatch between player expectation and product reality. A secondary D1 driver is the visual style: the dark, minimal aesthetic may feel underwhelming to players accustomed to the higher-fidelity visuals of competing titles.

D7 — Day 7 Churn

By day 7, bot-heavy matchmaking becomes the dominant frustration. User reviews describe lobbies overwhelmingly populated by bots, which removes the strategic tension that makes Bullet Echo's line-of-sight mechanic compelling. A secondary D7 driver is the power gap that emerges around the 300+ power threshold, creating a progression wall that coincides with the end of the accessible early game.

D30 — Day 30 Churn

Long-term churn is driven by pay-to-win power imbalance and persistent bug issues following updates. With 143 combined pay-to-win mentions and 46 bug and crash mentions across TR and US reviews, Bullet Echo's D30 churn profile is a dual technical and monetization problem. Veteran players with 3 to 5 years of playtime describe progress being repeatedly undermined by updates that reset the competitive landscape in favor of spenders.

Tacticool +

D1 — Day 1 Churn

The primary D1 churn driver is expectation mismatch combined with an overly long tutorial flow. Tacticool's App Store visuals create a strong FPS impression, while the actual game is a top-down third-person shooter with a notably slower pace. The tutorial guides players through 4 to 5 consecutive forced missions, keeping new players locked in a structured loop for 10 to 15 minutes before they can play freely.

D7 — Day 7 Churn

By day 7, the pay-to-win structure and server instability become the dominant frustrations. With 143 combined pay-to-win mentions across TR and US reviews, players begin to feel that competitive success is determined by spending rather than skill. Tacticool also has the highest server-related complaint count of all five games (37 combined mentions), meaning players face frequent disconnections on top of the pay-to-win dynamic.

D30 — Day 30 Churn

Long-term churn is driven by update-induced progress loss. Multiple reviewers with 3 to 5 years of playtime and level 1000+ characters describe a recent update that effectively reset their loadouts, making years of progression feel worthless. This is among the most severe D30 churn patterns in this analysis: alienating the highest-engagement segment through a single product decision.

Modern Strike +

D1 — Day 1 Churn

The primary D1 churn driver is a fragmented onboarding experience. Modern Strike requires an additional in-app download after the initial install, adding a friction step before any value has been delivered. Once in the game, first matches are excessively easy: enemies die in a single hit regardless of weapon. Scoring 25 kills without dying once is not empowering, it removes any sense of challenge. A secondary D1 driver is the post-match UX: when a match ends, the screen presents only an exit option rather than a clear play-again button, breaking the momentum at the highest-engagement point in a session.

D7 — Day 7 Churn

By day 7, technical instability becomes the dominant churn driver. Modern Strike has the highest combined bug, crash, and server complaint count of all five games, with 90 bug mentions and 43 server and lag mentions. IAP pressure is a secondary D7 driver: players who are already frustrated by technical issues are less willing to spend on a product that feels unstable.

D30 — Day 30 Churn

Long-term churn is driven by technical failures and trust erosion. Multiple long-term reviews describe losing months of progress due to account issues and forced reinstalls requesting payment information. One reviewer described playing for months and spending on VIP passes, only to have everything disappear with no way to recover or contact support. This represents the most severe form of D30 churn: not gradual disengagement, but a complete and irreversible break in player trust.

Case Study — Question Three

Q3 — A/B Testing Suggestions

Test 01

IAP Strategy Overhaul

The current IAP approach floods players with 5 to 10 purchase prompts at session start, training them to dismiss offers without reading. This test restructures the entire IAP experience into three stages: no offers shown during the first three sessions; a single well-timed and relevant offer introduced after the third session; and from that point forward, behavior-triggered offers shown at moments of peak engagement such as after a strong match performance. As an additional variant, a loss aversion mechanic can be introduced: give new players a free starter kit after their first match, set it to expire after a few sessions, and present the first IAP offer as a way to keep what they already have.

Target KPIs: D1 retention rate, session start completion rate, offer view-through rate, IAP conversion rate, ARPPU, D7 and D30 retention, LTV.

Hypothesis: Reducing offer frequency and improving timing will decrease short-term conversion volume but increase long-term ARPPU and retention by building player trust rather than depleting it.

Test 02

Bot Composition

Currently, bots are equipped with high-tier gear, causing players who lose to attribute their defeat to equipment rather than skill. This test replaces gear-advantage bots with skill-based bots: opponents that can defeat the player through superior positioning and decision-making rather than superior equipment. When a player loses to a skilled but modestly equipped bot, the natural response is "I need to play better" rather than "I need to spend more." The calibration threshold is the most critical variable: bots that are too difficult will increase frustration, while bots that are too easy will feel meaningless.

Target KPIs: D7 retention, session length, churn rate among non-payers, IAP conversion rate.

Hypothesis: Skill-based bot behavior will reduce pay-to-win perception, improve retention among non-paying players, and paradoxically increase IAP conversion by making players feel that skill rather than spending determines outcomes.

Test 03

League-Based Auto-Aim Reduction

Auto-aim is currently uniform across all leagues, creating a ceiling on skill development. This test introduces a small, fixed reduction in auto-aim sensitivity with each league advancement, communicated transparently to players as a feature of progression rather than a penalty. Players in the same league are matched only against each other to ensure a level playing field. The framing matters: "as you advance, the game becomes more real" positions the reduction as a reward and a mark of skill rather than a restriction.

Target KPIs: D30 retention, session length by league tier, league progression rate, churn rate by league tier.

Hypothesis: Gradual auto-aim reduction tied to league progression will increase long-term engagement by raising the skill ceiling and making advanced play feel more meaningful and earned.

Test 04

Home Screen Simplification

The current home screen contains an excessive number of IAP-driven elements that overwhelm new players and prevent them from exploring and personalizing the game. This test introduces a simplified home screen where IAP-pushing elements are significantly reduced, the player character is given more visual prominence and becomes a natural entry point into customization, and shop content is moved into a dedicated store area. Within this test, the loading screen between matches is also personalized based on the player's last match performance, showing contextual tips rather than generic season-focused content.

Target KPIs: Customization interaction rate, settings exploration rate, D7 and D30 retention, IAP conversion rate, session start completion rate.

Hypothesis: Reducing visual noise and IAP pressure on the home screen will increase player exploration and personalization behavior, and players who personalize the game will exhibit significantly higher long-term retention.

Test 05

Matchmaking by Spend Tier

Currently, free-to-play players are regularly matched against heavy spenders, making the competitive gap visible and demoralizing from the first week of play. This test separates matchmaking pools by spend tier, ensuring that non-paying players compete primarily against other non-paying players. This does not eliminate pay-to-win mechanics but removes their most damaging visible effect: the feeling that spending is the only path to competitive relevance.

Target KPIs: D7 and D30 retention among non-payers, session length, churn rate by spend tier, IAP conversion rate among free-to-play players.

Hypothesis: Separating matchmaking pools by spend tier will significantly improve retention among non-paying players and may increase IAP conversion by making the game feel fair enough to invest in.

Case Study — Question Four

Q4 — Piggy Bank Feature

✓ Why It Works in Casual

💡

Endowment Effect

Players perceive accumulated currency as earned through their own effort, valuing it more than an equivalent store offer.

💰

Value Comparison

A full piggy bank visibly outperforms standard shop offerings, making the purchase feel like a smart financial decision.

✓

Simplicity

A single currency type makes the value proposition immediately clear.

✗ Why It Struggles in Mid-Core

⚠

Currency Complexity

Multiple currency types create confusion around what the bank contains and what it is actually worth.

⚔

Competitive Scrutiny

Mid-core players evaluate every purchase through the lens of competitive advantage, not just value.

🛡

Manipulation Sensitivity

Mid-core players are significantly more sensitive to feeling manipulated — in a game already carrying heavy IAP pressure, a poorly implemented piggy bank can deepen distrust rather than build it.

Approach A

Battle Pass Integration

Ties the piggy bank to an existing system rather than introducing it as a standalone IAP. Inspired by World of Tanks Blitz, which integrates its vaults into the battle pass structure, the piggy bank in Critical Strike would fill passively throughout a season and become claimable only for players who hold the premium battle pass at season end. This removes the manipulative feeling entirely: the player is receiving an added benefit of a system they have already invested in.

Approach B

Skill-Linked Active Accumulation

Transforms the piggy bank from a passive accumulation system into an active, skill-linked experience. Specific high-skill actions fill the bank: a grenade kill, a headshot, and finishing a match as MVP each contribute a meaningful amount, while standard kills do not contribute at all. Each contributing action triggers a small, non-intrusive animation in the corner of the screen. This creates a significantly stronger endowment effect than passive accumulation, because the player can directly trace the bank's contents back to specific moments of skill.

Case Study — Question Five

Q5 — Games That Kept Me Playing

♟

Chess

"The skill ceiling is effectively infinite and every single game presents a completely different problem to solve."

Chess is not a game I picked up, it is something I have been part of since I was six years old. What makes it impossible to put down permanently is not a reward system, a battle pass, or a progression loop: it is the fact that the skill ceiling is effectively infinite and every single game presents a completely different problem to solve. There is no "finishing" chess. At every level, there is always something new to learn, a pattern you have not seen, a mistake you did not know you were making. That is a retention mechanic that no free-to-play system has ever replicated.

Infinite skill ceiling
🃏

Balatro

"The game has a genuine personality. It is sardonic, self-aware, and quietly confident in its own strangeness."

Balatro came into my life differently. I downloaded it because I was fascinated by the story behind it: a game built by a single developer that became one of the most talked-about releases of the year. That origin pulled me in before I had played a single hand. What kept me was something harder to manufacture: the game has a genuine personality. It is sardonic, self-aware, and quietly confident in its own strangeness. It takes poker, a game with its own centuries of cultural weight, and rebuilds it into something that feels completely original by layering in roguelike mechanics, card synergies, and escalating absurdity. The fact that something this conceptually simple became this successful is, from a product perspective, one of the best arguments for the power of a strong core loop and a distinctive identity over feature complexity.

Genuine personality + one more run loop

What both games share, and what I find most instructive as someone looking at mid-core retention, is that neither of them pressures me. No push notifications pulling me back, no energy systems punishing absence, no IAP popups eroding the experience. I open them when I want them, and that freedom is exactly why I keep coming back. The lesson for mid-core games is not that monetization or engagement mechanics are wrong, but that the most durable retention is built on players genuinely wanting to return, not being nudged, reminded, or pressured into it.
